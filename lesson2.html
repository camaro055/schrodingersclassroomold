1-D Schrödinger Equation

The Wave Function

Wave functions contain all the information that can be known about a system and are used to represent the state of a physical system. Thus a wave function, generally speaking, has real parts and imaginary parts In coordinate space a particle is repersented by the wave funtion of the position of the particle at a given time:

In 1-Dimension ψ=(x,t)
2-Dimensions ψ=(x,y,t)
3-Dimentions ψ=(x,y,z,t)

Even particles within N dimensions (where N can equal 1,2,3...,∞) can be repersented with the use of a wave equation:

ψ=(x1,x2,...,xN,y1,y2,...,yN,z1,z2,...,zN,t)

Normalization of the Wave Function

As discussed in the Introduciton to Quantum Mechanics lesson, particles are measured by probability. Probability is repersented by a real number between 0 and 1. How ever a measurement of probability 0 is an impossible outcome, since the particle must be somewhere, and a probability of 1 is a certian outcome, meaning that there is a 100% chance the particle is there. Looking at the simplest Probability function, using a 1-D wave function, normalization of a wave function means finding the form of the wave function that makes the statement:

P(x,t)=∫ |ψ(x,t)|2 dx = 1

|ψ(x,t)|2 can also be written as
ψ(x,t)*ψ(x,t) or ρ(x,t)

True.

Essential this means normalizing is finding the exact for of ψ that provides a probability that the particle is found somewhere in the space is 1. This sometimes means solving for some constant related to the above constraint.

Time-Independent Schrödinger Equation

Now with the understanding of probability and normalization of wave functions we can introduce Schrödinger's Time-independent equation:

The Schrödinger equation also sometime known as Schrödingers wave equation is a partial differential equation. The equation essential is:

Kinetic Energy + Potential Energy = Total Energy (Energy Conservation)

It uses energy conservation inorder to find out information about an electrons behaviour and its bond to a nucleus. The way Schrödingers equation is able to do this is by allowing us to calculate the electron's wave function. By calculating the electron's wave equation we are now able to obtain the electron's energy levels, quantum numbers, and the shapes and orientations of orbitals which all characterize the electrons in an atom. In the equation above the variables given represent:

ℏ = h/2π where h=6.626x10-34 (J s)(Planck's Constant)
m = electron mass (9.109x10-31kg)
∇2=Laplacian Operator (Laplacian Operator)
ψ = the wave function
V = the potential energy
E = the energy eigenvalue (Energy eigenvalue)
r = x (the position variable)

With the Time-Independet Schrödinger Equation and the rest of the knowledge on wave functions from above we are now ready to tackle the Particle in a Box problem introduced in Lesson 2.
